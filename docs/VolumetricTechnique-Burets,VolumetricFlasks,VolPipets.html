Part 2: Using A Buret and Graduated Cylinder

The student uses two common volumetric devices in the chemistry lab.

Graduated Cylinder

The graduated cylinder is one of the most common devices in a chemistry lab. The liquid is poured into the cylinder to the correct volume marks which are part of the construction of the cylinder. Initially the bulk of the desired volume can be added by a beaker. But as the chemist gets to the final desired mark, a small squeeze bulb transfer pipet can be used to add liquid drop by drop to the final mark.

  1. 100 mL graduated cylinder. A budget plastic 100 mL cylinder is used for introductory labs because it avoids the frequent breakage that occurs in this setting. The smallest division markings will be 1.0 mL. The precision of this cylinder should be 10% of the smallest division marking: so the precision is to 0.1 mL. The scientist should record measured volumes to X.X mL (a tenth of a mL) in the lab
  2. 10 mL graduated cylinder. This is made of glass but has fittings on it to avoid shocks causing breakage

Preparation of Cylinders For Use. The best way of preparing a cylinder is to wash it a few times first with deionized (or distilled) water, filling fully. Then fill the cylinder up from 20-40% of capacity with the solution whose volume you will measure: this amount is will be a pre-wash wastage that will make your measured out solution as pure as possible; if you don't have enough volume for the pre-wash and the solution you need to measure out, you can forgo the pre-wash, although it is nice to do. The pre-wash is usually more desirable for narrow bore, smaller capacity cylinders (like 10 mL)

Use of Cylinders. Pour in the solution to be measured close to the final desired volume. Then add drop-by-drop with transfer pipet to the mark. Note that sometimes the filling of the cylinder might start with hold it at an angle and adding liquid to the top of the inner wall to prevent drops from hitting the liquid surface directly to prevent foaming, bubble formation, cavitation, which cause lead to reading the meniscus of the liquid.

Buret

Unlike the graduated cylinder, which used preparatively, the buret is used purely analytically. The volumetric precision of the buret is X.XX mL (one-hundredth of a mL) whereas the 100 mL graduated cylinder is to X.X mL (one-tenth of a mL). The most important lesson in the use of the buret in titration is that because the precision of measurement is higher, so the result is more precise.

Filling the buret. With or without a funnel, the student can use a small beaker to fill the buret from the spout. It is vital that if the student used a funnel that it be removed. Funnels with narrow bore stems can retain fluid through strong cohesion, and this fluid can come out during the handling (can cause minor jostling) of the buret in titration. Fluid can leak out from funnel stem through capillary action aided by the downward force caused by gravity.

  • Initial filling. No effort should be made to fill the buret with titrant to exactly the 0.00 mL mark. It is a waste of time and demonstrates poor technique. Better is to fill to a little more than the 0.00 mL and less than the 3.00 mL mark. Once filled, get the precise initial buret reading and record it.
