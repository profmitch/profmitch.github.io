On the Practice Exam Questions, #61 was a chemical equation balancing problem

image.png

The indicated correct answer was wrong. (This should be an indicator that A.I. gets it wrong and horribly, as this was an A.I.-generated question that was not properly vetted--I have to take responsibility for that.)

This is the thinking process for balancing:

(i) Start with balancing N atoms. There is one of the left (of the arrow). There is one on the right. Good so far. Move on to the next atom.
(ii) Proceed to the H atom. Why? Because you are always better off when you start balancing compounds that have few presence on each side of the arrow. The O atom is present in 3 compounds, one on the left, two on the right. The H atom is seen in only two compounds, one of the left, one on the right. Use that logic.
So there are 3 H atoms on the left and 2 H atoms on the right. Time to go to work. With that kind of imbalance, you do something like the criss-cross technique for subscripts of compounds. OK: Put a 2 on the left for NH3, put a 3 on the right for H2O. That creates 6 H atoms on both sides. (See the trick?)
(iii) Because you added coefficients, you now have to go back to Step (i) where you evaluated N atoms, because you changed those. You created 2 N atoms on the left, so now have to put 2 N atoms on the right. Let's write our equation so far:
  2 NH3 +  __ O2 --> 2 NO + 3 H2O
(iv) N and H atom counts on both sides look good. Now to the O atoms
(v) You have 2 O atoms on left and 2 + 3 = 5 O atoms on right.  The best approach is to deal with the compound with the ODD NUMBER of O atoms, which is H2O.
(vi) Multiply by 2 the H2O -->  2 NH3 + __ O2 --> 2 NO + 6 H2O
(vii) Adjust now for NH3 to balance its 6 H atoms to the 12 atoms of H2O on right:   4 NH3 + __ O2  --> 2 NO + 6 H2O
(viii) Now adjust for the change of N atoms on the left to the NO molecules on the right:  4 NH3 + __O2 --> 4 NO + 6 H2O
(ix) Everything on the N and H atoms looks balanced now. It only remains to check the O atoms. Remember at step (v) we were faced with an ODD NUMBER of O atoms: do we have them now?  There are 4 + 6 O atoms on the right. Great!! An even number, which we can balance on the left with the O2 molecule, which is 5 O2 molecules!
Final balanced equation:    4 NH3 + 5 O2 -->  4 NO + 6 H2O.  The answer choice SHOULD have been   4, 5, 4, 6. 

Keep in mind that 94.2% of chemical equation balancing logic operations are not so complicated. This was obviously chosen to be complicated and test your logic

But you would have figured that out.

Review it carefully since it contains an example of how you should do your lab report. It shows how to calculate and also how to make sure your numerical values have the correct significant digits!

If you give an energy (q) value of 1348.3 J  or 0.93499 kJ, something is wrong. Your temperature difference values alone will narrow your precision to two, and at most three significant digits. If you have a temp difference of say –2,3°C, that's only two significant digits. If you have a temp difference of say 12.7°C, that's only 3 sig digits. Those energy values for 1300 J or 0.935 kJ or even 0.94 kJ are much more reasonable. No energy calculation is THAT precise where it has 5 or 8 digits (!!)
