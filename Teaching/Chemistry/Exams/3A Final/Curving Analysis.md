## 🔍 Key Considerations

### 1. Scaling Issue
- The national exam has **70 questions**, yours has **50**.
- A direct comparison isn’t possible without **rescaling**.
- Example: A student scoring 25/50 = 50%. On a 70-question scale, that’s equivalent to 35/70.

👉 First step: Convert all your students’ scores to a **70-question equivalent** by multiplying by \( \frac{70}{50} = 1.4 \).

---

### 2. Guessing Floor
- On a 70-question exam, random guessing yields ~17–18 correct.
- On a 50-question exam, random guessing yields ~12–13 correct.
- This means your exam’s “floor” is lower, so weaker students may appear slightly worse when scaled.

---

### 3. Norms vs. Local Curve
- **National norms**: Useful if you want to benchmark your class against a national population.
- **Local curve**: More consistent with how you’ve treated midterms. Keeps grading philosophy aligned across assessments.
- Mixing approaches (norms for final, curve for midterms) risks confusing students and creating fairness issues.

---

## 🛠️ Options for You

### Option A: **Transform to National Grade Scale**
1. Rescale each student’s score to a 70-question equivalent.
2. Use the ExamNorms table to map to percentile and grade.
   - Example: Student with 28/50 → 39.2/70 → round to 39 → Grade = 75 (C).
3. This gives you a nationally benchmarked grade distribution.

✅ Pros: Transparent, defensible, nationally standardized.  
❌ Cons: May not align with your midterm grading philosophy.

---

### Option B: **Curve Locally (Ignore Norms)**
1. Work only with your 50-question scores.
2. Decide on a curve (e.g., mean = C, top 10% = A).
3. Apply consistently across midterms and finals.

✅ Pros: Consistency across your course.  
❌ Cons: Less connection to national benchmarks.

---

### Option C: **Hybrid Approach**
- Use national norms as a **reference**, but design your own curve.
- Example: See how your class compares to national percentiles, then adjust cutoffs to match your course’s grading philosophy.
- This way, you avoid harsh mismatches but still benefit from the external benchmark.
