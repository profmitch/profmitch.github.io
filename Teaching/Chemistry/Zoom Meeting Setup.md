# 🎬 Step‑by‑Step: A Reliable Zoom Teaching Setup Using Only Your Phone + Household Items

Absolutely — let’s build you a **practical, stable, zero‑cost teaching setup** you can use tomorrow. I’ll walk you through it like you’re assembling a small lab station: clear steps, no fluff, no purchases required.

---

## 🧩 Part 1 — Positioning Your Phone as Your Webcam/Mic

### **Step 1: Choose your “teaching position”**

Pick one of these:

- **Sitting at a desk** (best for long sessions)
- **Standing at a counter** (best if you gesture a lot)

### **Step 2: Stabilize the phone**

Use whichever option fits your space:

#### **Option A — Cup‑holder mount in a mug**

- Put a heavy mug on your desk.
- Insert your car cup‑holder mount into the mug.
- Tighten or wedge it with a napkin so it doesn’t wobble.
- Clip your phone in.

This gives you a surprisingly stable, adjustable stand.

#### **Option B — Book stack + leaning phone**

- Stack 3–5 books.
- Lean the phone against a vertical object (another book, a jar).
- Adjust angle until your face is centered.

#### **Option C — DIY tripod**

- Two tall objects (cans, jars, stacked books)
- Lay a ruler or board across them
- Place the phone on top, camera facing down or forward

This becomes important in Part 2.

### **Step 3: Lighting**

- Put a lamp **behind the phone**, not behind you.
- Aim for light on your face or writing surface.
- Avoid overhead-only lighting (shadows).

### **Step 4: Audio check**

Your phone’s mic is usually excellent.

- In Zoom → **Settings → Audio → Test Mic**
- Speak at normal teaching volume
- Adjust distance: 1–2 feet away is ideal

## ✍️ Part 2 — Creating a “Document Camera” for Real‑Time Writing

This is the most important part for chemistry, math, or diagrams.

### **Step 1: Build the overhead rig**

Use the DIY tripod from above:

- Two tall objects (books, jars, cans)
- Ruler or cutting board across the top
- Phone resting on it, camera pointing down

You now have a **free overhead camera**.

### **Step 2: Prepare your writing surface**

- White printer paper or a notebook
- Dark pen or marker (blue/black)
- Keep the page centered under the camera

### **Step 3: Join Zoom from two devices**

- **Computer**: join as host (audio ON, video optional)
- **Phone**: join as a second device (audio OFF, video ON)

On the phone:

- Tap **Share → Camera**
- Choose **Back camera**
- Angle it downward at your paper

Students now see your hand and writing in real time.

### **Step 4: Adjust clarity**

- Raise/lower the phone until writing is crisp
- Tilt slightly if glare appears
- Keep your hand out of the way when possible

This setup is shockingly effective and costs nothing.

## 🧪 Part 3 — Using a Physical Whiteboard (Optional)

If you have a small whiteboard:

- Hang it behind you or prop it up
- Angle the phone to capture it
- Use thick markers
- Stand close enough that writing is legible

This works, but the document‑camera method is usually clearer.

---

## 📄 Part 4 — Sharing Drawings or Diagrams Without Writing Live

If you prefer to prepare diagrams:

### **Step 1: Draw on paper**

Use a pen or marker.

### **Step 2: Take a photo with your phone**

### **Step 3: Share in Zoom**

- Share screen → choose the photo
- Or upload to Zoom’s whiteboard and annotate

This is great for reaction mechanisms, tables, or anything you want to reuse.

---

## 🧭 Part 5 — Running the Actual Class Smoothly

### **Before class**

- Open Zoom on your computer
- Join from your phone as a second device
- Set phone to **Do Not Disturb**
- Clean your writing surface
- Have extra paper ready

### **During class**

- Switch between:
  - **Your face** (phone forward-facing)
  - **Your writing** (phone downward-facing)
- Use the computer for:
  - Screensharing slides
  - Monitoring chat
  - Letting students unmute

### **After class**

- Save any whiteboard images if needed
- Take photos of your handwritten notes for students
