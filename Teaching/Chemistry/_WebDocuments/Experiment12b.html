There are two parts. Part I is for you to determine the exact concentration of the NaOH reagent that you will use then to determine, in Part II, the exact concentration of citric acid in a soft drink, which is the ultimate goal of the experiment.

Part I: Determinining Exact Concentration of NaOH Titrant

Understand what you are doing in Part I. The supply list says "NaOH (˜0.1 M)" will be used (image below). That means when you determine what the concentration is, it should be close to 0.1 M. It should not be twice or more than that concentration.

Note that in the Example Data Analysis provided with the PDF of the PowerPoint it was shown how you use dissolved oxalic acid dihydrate (\(\mathrm{C_2H_2O_4\;\bullet 2\;H_2O} \), or more descriptively \(\mathrm{HOOCCOOH\;\bullet\;2\;H_2O }\)) which has two protons (acid groups) that must be neutralized, and thus requires 2 moles NaOH to neutralize 1 mole of oxalic acid dihydrate (OAA) to neutralize (see #1 in image below).
The table for Part I is completed doing three trials. (Why three trials? See below.) The average of all trials that are properly titrated—in other words, we exclude trials that are overtitrated (too much titrant used, making it too intense a pink color)—is taken. You should know how to compute an average: \[ \frac{0.102 + 0.0979 + 0.103}{3} = 0.101 \text{ mol/L} = 0.101 M\] This is shown in #2 in the image below.
That computed average value above is then written in that space at the top of the page where Part II determination is to be made. See the red oval with arrow pointing to it in the image below.

Some of you wrote in the value "0.101 M" which was the value of the Example Data Analysis. That is not your value. Your value is the concentration of NaOH that you determined, indicated in molar (M).

You will use YOUR value in your Part II calculations to determine the moles of citric acid (CA), NOT with the 0.101 mol/L value from the Example Data Analysis. See in the image below where the exact concentration of NaOH, determined in Part I titration, is used (value circled in red).
Again your Part I-determined concentration for NaOH is written in the Part II table (see #3). It is NOT the "0.101 M" value of the Example Data Analysis. But YOUR value.

But if Trial 1 as okay for you, do not write that in. The average value (#4 in the image below) was computed using the data from Trials #2 through #4.

Finally some of you may not be aware how to calculate the Average Molarity of 7-Up® and the "Grams Citric Acid in one can". The average molarity of 7-Up® is just found by dividing the 6-Down molarity by 10. That's it. For the Grams 7-Up® in one can:

\( \textbf{average molarity (mol/L)} \times \textbf{volume of can (L)} \times \textbf{citric acid molar mass (g/mol)} \) \[ \frac{5.289 \times 10^{-3} \cancel{\text{ mol citric acid}}}{1 \cancel{\text{ L soft drink}}} \times \frac{0.355 \cancel{\text{ L soft drink}}}{1} \times \frac{192.124 \text{ g citric acid}}{1 \cancel{\text{ mol citric acid}}} \] \[ = 0.361 \text{ g citric acid} \]
