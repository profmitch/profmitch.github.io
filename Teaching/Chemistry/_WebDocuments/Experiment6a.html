Lewis Structures

  • Carbon (C) and Hydrogen (H) atoms will not have nonbonding (lone) pairs of electrons. Oxygen (O) atoms in neutral (nonionic) molecules will pretty much have two lone pairs. Sulfur (S) is in the same group and will also show similar behavior. Nitrogen (N) atoms in nonionic molecules typically have one lone pair, and the same for its group member phosphorus (P).
  • The halogens (F, Cl, Br, I) will invariably have three lone pairs in their Lewis structures. It is easy to forget to show that.

Geometries

There are two kinds of geometries described for molecules: Electron Group Geometry and Molecular Geometry. The geometries focus on a central atom, which can be any element other than hydrogen, since hydrogen will only ever bond covalently to one atom.

  • Electron Group Geometry (EGG).
    There are TWO features involved in this geometry. The first feature is all the atoms that bond to the central atom. The second feature is all the nonbonding (lone) pairs that belong to the central atoms.

    If two electron groups—they are either atoms or lone pairs—are bonded to the central atom, then the molecule is going to be linear as to EGG. If three electron groups, the EGG will always be trigonal planar. If there are four electron groups, the high probability is for a tetrahedral geometry.

  • Molecular Geometry.
    There is only ONE feature in this geometry: it is atoms only and nonbonding (lone) pairs of electrons are not to be considered as in EGG.

    If there are two atoms bonded to the central atom, then it is going to be linear if the EGG is two groups and linear. It will be bent or angled also for two groups and if the EGG is four groups and tetrahedral. It will be trigonal planar if there are three atoms and EGG is three groups and also trigonal planar. It will be tetrahedral if there are four atoms and the EGG is four groups and also tetrahedral.
